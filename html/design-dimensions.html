Design Dimensions

Intro'

Broadly speaking, a designer's function is to take information about a design problem, garner some ideas, then assess and test them until a good ideas emerge.

Design dimensions - also known as laws, principles, perspectives and so on - are the many conceptual frameworks designers adopt when creating and assessing their ideas.

Below are some examples, note that some of these may have overlapping content, or be broader than others:

  • Usability
  • Trust
  • Appeal
  • Convention
  • Aesthetics
  • Signifiers
  • Physical context

These dimensions are important: someone unaware of these would find a design problem enigmatic. You could even argue that they are not a designer at all.

If a design idea were a break-board, design dimensions are both the hands that set them up, and the many fists attempting to break them. Good ideas get broken very rarely.

An example: Order

One dimension of design I recently found myself considering was Order. By order, I mean the sequential order of things: A to B to C.

Order is rarely considered explicitly; in life, the 'correct' sequential order is often obvious: I put on my socks before my shoes, I select the washing cycle before I start the washing machine.

As with any dimension of design, failing to consider order runs the risk of locking ourselves into sub-optimal design decisions: we reach local maxima.

I place particular emphasis on order specifically because it is fundamental, and often assumed early on in the design process.

What about user-testing?

You're right. User-testing is real, and design dimensions aren't!

Design dimensions are mere concepts which frame and describe reality, they're evolving constructs intended to make solving design problems easier.
It can therefore be said that design dimensions aren't inherently canonical - although some seem pretty close.

This is why the ultimate in design practice is user-testing: if a user-test adequately reflects reality, then they are effectively applying every possible dimension of design at once - including those as yet undescribed!

"Just user-test, then!" I hear you cry. Yes, that's the spirit. I'm sure we all think that every design team should be user-testing more. However, lets not forget that user-testing doth not a designer make.

The purpose of design dimensions

The case for utilising design dimensions is twofold: to avoid 'rediscovering fire', and to support the creative process.
