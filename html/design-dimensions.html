Design Dimensions

Design work is messy.

Intro'

Good designs are often the result of what I like to call 'idea-thrashing'.
Broadly speaking, a designer takes in swathes of information about a design problem, then creates and assesses their ideas.
By the end, the best ideas are those left relatively unscathed.

Design dimensions - or principles - are the conceptual frameworks designers use to help create and assess their ideas against the information available.

A design problem, its various factors, and some possible design ideas are enigmatic to someone lacking these concepts. In fact, you could argue that they are not a designer at all.

If a design idea were a break-board, design dimensions are both the hands that set them up, and the many fists attempting to break them.

User-testing?

You're right. User-testing is real, and design dimensions aren't!

Design dimensions are mere concepts which frame and describe reality, they're evolving constructs intended to make solving design problems easier.
It can therefore be said that design dimensions aren't inherently canonical - although some seem pretty close.

This is why the ultimate in design practice is user-testing: if a user-test adequately reflects reality, then they in effect are applying every possible design dimensions at once - including those as yet undescribed.

"Just user-test, then!" I hear you cry. Yes, we should probably be user-testing way more. However, user-testing doth not a designer make.

Justifying design dimensions

The case for utilising design dimensions is twofold: to avoid rediscovering fire, and to support the creative process.
